About Me

I’m Adrian Macadangdang, a passionate IT professional with experience in system support, cybersecurity, and training. But let's be honest—you've already seen my technical knowledge and credentials in the sections above. I'm not here to repeat my resume. This is about telling you who I am as a person.

Until the age of six, I grew up in Norway. We spent a lot of time outdoors and socializing, which is probably why I still love nature so much. Academic subjects weren’t much of a concern back then. But when my family moved back to the U.S., school was suddenly very different—and I couldn’t read at all, even though it was expected. With no other choice, I learned to read in two weeks. By the end of the year, I had the highest reading score in the class.

I don't have a college degree. Before IT, I worked as a phlebotomist with no technical background. Still, I saw myself in tech. I had no clue how I'd get there, but I knew I'd figure it out. I settled on IT and started studying for the A+ certification during the slow hours of my night shift and in the mornings before work. One day, before my shift started, my supervisor frantically pulled me into her office. "I think there's an open IT position here!" she said. I was skeptical because I wasn't qualified, but she told me I wasn’t allowed to leave until I applied. She even wrote a glowing letter of recommendation to the department manager and followed up with a call to the IT manager. I'll never be able to thank her enough for believing in me.

Thanks to my supervisor, I landed an interview for that IT position. Across the table sat the head of IT, a Senior Systems Engineer, and another Systems Engineer who had started on the same helpdesk team I was interviewing for before getting promoted. The Senior Engineer asked, "So Adrian, I know you've mentioned you don't really have any technical knowledge, so we'll keep that part of the interview brief. Do you know what Active Directory is?" I shook my head no. He gave a brief nod. "So no hands-on experience then?" Again, I shook my head no. Somehow, the rest of the interview went really well—and I landed the position despite my lack of technical knowledge.

Oh. That's why I got the job. When I joined, there were only three people supporting an entire enterprise of over two thousand users. Normally there were five, but due to a promotion and another employee’s failing health, they were badly short-staffed. To put it bluntly, they were desperate. As a result, training was... minimal. I was thrown into as deep an end as you can imagine. Then, a month or two in, we lost another teammate and dropped back to three out of five. Three people (well, 2.5, really). Over 2,000 employees to support. One month of "training" with no prior experience. Needless to say, it was a massive challenge. But as I became more capable and contributed more to the team, I loved every second of it.

I love looking back on that time, because it was the start of such a wonderful journey. So much has changed since then—not just in terms of my technical knowledge and competency, but in who I am as a person. My ability to solve problems, work in a team, achieve objectives, and build strong relationships all grew tremendously. It was a phenomenal learning experience and the perfect growth environment for who I was then. It's the kind of opportunity I'll always be looking for, and I hope I continue to find it.
